# G2E_WR_002 Informative Writing Curriculum Review

## Executive summary

All 50 level-bank questions in **G2E_WR_002: Write Informative/Explanatory Text** were reviewed and rewritten. The revised package now gives Grade 2 students focused practice introducing a topic, selecting relevant facts, connecting ideas, and writing a concluding statement. It stays within the authored informative-writing scope and does not add narrative, opinion, research-report, or upper-grade composition requirements.

The review replaces repeated generated frames with natural classroom directions, varied nonfiction contexts, teachable explanations, realistic misconceptions, and question-specific three-step scaffolds. Mixed review now measures transfer with ten new topics rather than repeating focused items.

## Original curriculum defects

The baseline had substantial publication blockers:

- All 35 displayed-answer questions placed the correct answer first and most offered only three choices.
- Ten prompts were exact duplicates, eleven normalized answer sets were duplicates, and all ten Mixed prompts repeated focused prompts.
- Six Mixed answer sets were copied from focused banks.
- Every question reused the same generic hint ladder, explanation, and feedback.
- Topic introductions repeated the frame “___ are interesting to learn about,” while distractors such as “Maybe” and “Wow” were implausible.
- Fact questions often contrasted a fact only with broad opinions such as “the best” and “everyone loves,” making answers conspicuous rather than diagnostic.
- Paragraph models repeated one mechanical structure and conclusion across topics, including awkward capitalization of “Farmers Markets.”
- Explanations said only that an answer “fits the writing target,” without teaching why it introduced, supported, connected, or concluded the text.

No answer-key factual contradiction was found, but the keys were not publication-ready because their positions were entirely predictable and several activities did not display four choices.

## Educational improvements

- Level 1 now distinguishes a true topic introduction from a narrow detail, personal experience, vague reference, or opinion.
- Level 2 asks students to identify checkable, relevant facts and distinguish them from preferences and experiences.
- Level 3 teaches meaningful relationships between facts: addition, time, example, and cause/result.
- Level 4 supplies manageable fact boxes and asks for an introduction, both relevant facts, linking words, and a conclusion.
- Mixed review interleaves these skills in unfamiliar contexts, including selecting conclusions and composing new paragraphs.
- Language, facts, workload, and sentence structure remain appropriate for Grade 2.

## Writing improvements

Directions now name the exact writing move and, for extended responses, identify the four expected paragraph parts. Model paragraphs vary their introductions, connections, and conclusions rather than teaching a single fill-in frame. Explanations identify the function of the correct sentence and clarify the misconception represented by alternatives. Each hint ladder progresses from attention, to application, to verification and uses the question's own topic or relationship.

The lesson model was also tightened to define informative writing accurately: introduce the topic, develop it with relevant facts, connect ideas, and conclude. Worked examples now demonstrate both planning and revision.

## Representative review examples

| Skill | Before | After | Why the revision is stronger |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Introduce a topic | “Which sentence names the topic butterflies?” | “A class is beginning an informative paragraph about sea turtles. Which sentence clearly introduces the topic?” | Establishes an authentic writing purpose and asks students to choose between realistic sentence roles. |
| Select facts | A fact competed with “the best” and “everyone loves.” | The bridge fact competes with a preference, a feeling, and a personal choice. | Distractors represent common fact/opinion confusion without being silly or impossible. |
| Link ideas | A blank competed with “Maybe” and “Wow.” | “A lighthouse has a bright signal light. ___, the light helps guide ships near a coast.” | Students must recognize a cause-and-result relationship, not merely spot the only transition. |
| Build a paragraph | Ten topics used the same “interesting to learn about” model and “Now you know” ending. | Fact boxes support varied paragraphs about butterflies, sunflowers, compost, the water cycle, and other topics. | Models informative organization while leaving room for age-appropriate composing. |
| Transfer | Mixed copied focused activities. | Mixed uses sled dogs, rain gauges, migration, sand dunes, park rangers, spiderwebs, glaciers, kites, penguin feathers, and bike helmets. | New material tests whether students can apply the writing concept independently. |

## Answer-position analysis

The baseline had 35 choice activities, with **A/B/C/D = 35/0/0/0** (100%/0%/0%/0%). The revision has 37 choice activities, with **A/B/C/D = 11/8/8/10** (29.7%/21.6%/21.6%/27.0%). Every position is within the 15–35% target. Each choice activity displays four normalized-unique choices and exactly one displayed key.

## Accessibility observations

- Prompts use direct verbs and short, concrete directions suitable for reading aloud.
- Question audio text is synchronized with revised prompts and answer audio with revised models.
- Meaning does not depend on color, sound, or visual position.
- Fact boxes and paragraph checklists reduce memory load for extended writing.
- Hints provide graduated support without revealing the answer in step one.
- Complete sentences and explicit relationship language support multilingual learners and students who benefit from language scaffolds.

## Interaction assessment

The package uses only existing `multiple_choice`, `short_response`, `sentence_completion`, and `writing_response` interactions. Strict schema validation, renderer output checks, and correct/incorrect evaluation checks cover every authored type. Existing `topic_detail_chart`, `fact_cards`, `sentence_builder`, `paragraph_builder`, and `writing_checklist` visual models remain supported. No shared renderer, evaluator, answer shuffling, or Skill World runtime changes were needed.
